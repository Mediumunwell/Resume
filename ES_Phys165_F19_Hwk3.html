In this homework you are being asked to use data collected that measured the
apparent luminosity of an astronomical source versus the redshift of the source.

The sources measured are something referred to as a "standard candle". That is, their
luminosity at the source is the same for all sources (actually there are some corrections used).

So, their apparent, or measured, luminosity here at earth should fall off with distance.
In fact, as we know, the luminosity should go down with

$L \propto 1/d^2 \;\;\;\; (1)$ .

Astronomers often represent brightness of an object by something called "magnitude". Where
1 unit of increased magnitude is

$\sqrt[5]{100} \approx 2.51 \;\;\;\; (2)$

times lower in brightness.

A little algebra on the two equations above yields a relation for the measured magnutude $m$ as a function of distance to be

$m(d) = M' + 5 \log_{10}d \;\;\;\; (3)$

where $M'$ is constant and related to the initial luminosity at the source location.

The data we are using actually is given as measured magnitude $m$ versus redshift $z$.

The "redshift" is a way to indicate distance by using how "redshifted" the light is. Where, because of the expansion of the universe and the fact that the farther away soemthing is, the faster it is going and therefore more redshifted. It is related to the Hubble constant constant among other things.

It turns out that the relation between the lumindosity distance $d_L$ to be used in equation 3 in terms of redshift is a function of cosmological parameters.

Puting it all together we can write equation 3 as:

$m(z) = M' + 5 \log_{10} d_L(z;\Omega_M,\Omega_\Lambda) \;\;\;\; (4)$

where $(\Omega_M,\Omega_\Lambda)$ Are cosmological parameters. We will use this full relation in Lab 1 for this class to fit high redshift data to get these parameters. More on that later!

For now we are using low redshift data where the $d_L(z;\Omega_M,\Omega_\Lambda)$ can be approximated by $d_L \approx z$ for small z. Then, we have for the relation of equation 4 that

$m(z) = M' + 5 \log_{10} d_L(z;\Omega_M,\Omega_\Lambda) \; \approx M' + 5 \log_{10}z \;\;\;\; (5)$
